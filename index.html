The Problem

Every year, an estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic waste enter our oceans. Much of this is poly(ethylene terephthalate) (PET)‚Äîused in bottles, packaging and other single‚Äêuse items‚Äîwhich persists for decades and fragments into microplastics, entering marine food-chains. If current trends continue, projections warn that by 2050 the oceans may contain more plastic by mass than fish. This scenario threatens marine ecosystems, food security, and public health.

Effects on marine life:
Marine debris harms hundreds of species; ingestion or entanglement is widespread. Coral reefs are especially vulnerable: studies show that corals entangled in plastic are far more likely to become diseased. Sea turtles, seabirds and whales routinely ingest plastic or become entangled, reducing survival and reproduction.

Plastic pollution doesn‚Äôt only harm wildlife‚Äîmicroplastics have been found in seafood, drinking water and even air. Without urgent intervention, the plastic crisis threatens both marine biodiversity and human well-being. PlastiCure exists to provide a scalable solution to this urgent problem.

Our Research

At PlastiCure, our research sits at the intersection of robotics, microbiology and synthetic biology. We leverage the bacterium Ideonella sakaiensis, known for its PETase and MHETase enzymes that degrade PET into smaller, biodegradable components. We develop genetically enhanced strains that increase enzyme secretion and optimise their activity in marine-like conditions.

Genome-editing platform: CRISPR‚ÄëCas9
Our team has reviewed and built upon publicly available research showing CRISPR-Cas9 can be used to edit genomes for biotechnological purposes, including therapy for genetic diseases. We use these advances to enhance I. sakaiensis for ocean plastic biodegradation, emphasising safety and ecological compatibility.

Technology

PlastiCure combines advanced robotics, AI and synthetic biology to deliver a precision solution for ocean plastic clean-up.

Our robotic platform uses cameras and AI models (MediaPipe, OpenCV) to scan and identify ocean plastics in real time. Once plastic is found, the robot deploys a controlled quantity of genetically enhanced Ideonella sakaiensis into a micro-enclosure, ensuring safety and targeted intervention. Sensors monitor local conditions and maintain strict containment protocols to protect ecosystems.
